2.3 Image Aberrations

In a homogenous medium the wavefront is spherical in shape. However, when the wavefront propagates through an optical system, it is deformed, and its shape is no longer spherical. As the rays are normal to the wavefront, they no longer converge to a sharp image point; i.e., the ideal image point as defined by central projection.

  • W/nW/nW/n wave deformation.
  • α\alphaα angular ray aberration
  • ϵ\epsilonϵ transverse ray aberration
  • lll longitudinal ray aberration.

For an axially symmetric system the aberration function, W(H⃗,ρ⃗)W(\vec{H},\vec{\rho})W(H,ρ​) , provides the geometrical wavefront deformation at the exit pupil as a function of the normalized field, H⃗\vec{H}H, and aperture, ρ⃗\vec{\rho}ρ​, vectors.

The field vector is located at the object plane and defines where a given ray originates from. The aperture vector defines the intersection of a given ray with the pupil plane. The aperture vector is usually located at the exit pupil plane, but it can also be located at the entrance pupil plane.

The aberration function, being a scalar, involves dot products of the field and aperture vectors, specifically H⃗⋅H⃗\vec{H} \cdot \vec{H}H⋅H, H⃗⋅ρ⃗\vec{H} \cdot \vec{\rho}H⋅ρ​, and ρ⃗⋅ρ⃗\vec{\rho} \cdot \vec{\rho}ρ​⋅ρ​. These dot products only depend on the magnitude of the vectors and on the cosine of the angle, ϕ\phiϕ, between them.

Equivalently, the aberration function provides the Optical Path Difference (OPD) between the OPL from the object to the wavefront at the exit pupil, and the OPL from the object point to the reference sphere. The aberration function is written to sixth-order of approximation as:

The order of an aberration term is given by 2⋅(j+m+n)2 \cdot (j + m + n)2⋅(j+m+n), which is always an even order.

The lower indices k,l,mk, l, mk,l,m in each coefficient indicate, respectively, the algebraic power of the field vector, the aperture vector, and the cosine of the angle ϕ\phiϕ between these vectors.

The fourth-order terms are often called the primary aberrations. The ten sixth-order terms can be divided into two groups. 2.5 Field of View and Relative Aperture

The relative aperture is defined as the ratio of the effective focal length EFL to the diameter of the entrance pupil DED_EDE​. Also known as F/#F/\#F/#, FNOFNOFNO, FFF-number, and focal ration FFF.

For lens systems that work at finite conjugates the effective relative aperture, sometimes referred to as the working F/#F/\#F/#, is given by F/#=(1−m)EFL/DEF/\# = (1-m)EFL/D_EF/#=(1−m)EFL/DE​, where mmm is the transverse magnification.

In a lens that is free from spherical aberration the numerical aperture (NANANA) is defined by NA=nsin⁡(θ)NA = n\sin(\theta)NA=nsin(θ) where n is the index of refraction and θ\thetaθ is the angle of the real marginal ray with the optical axis

Usually, the larger the field of view or the lower the F/#F/\#F/# is, the more difficult it is to design a lens.

2.8 Parity of the Aberrations and the Principle of Symmetry

The odd aberrations are coma and distortion. When there is some lens symmetry about the stop aperture, the odd aberrations tend to cancel, and this provides a mechanism to correct or mitigate the odd aberrations. This is known as the principle of symmetry about the stop.
